# About me

I was born in 1994 into a modest family in India. My father, a private school teacher, never earned much — but he earned something far more valuable: the respect and acknowledgment of everyone around him. My mother, a homemaker, held our family together with quiet strength. Growing up alongside my elder brother and younger sister, I learned early what resilience looks like.

I was a sickly child who spent most of his time at home. But being homebound turned into an unexpected gift — I became a self-learner. I was always in the top 1 or 2 in my class, not because I memorized textbooks, but because of a lesson that changed my life forever.

## The moment that changed everything

During a science exam, I was too sick to recall anything I had studied. I sat there, crying, convinced I would fail. Then my science teacher walked over and said the golden words:

> *"You are in my class. You remember what I taught you. Just write what you understand in your own words. I don't care about grammatical mistakes — if I can understand what you wrote, I will give you marks."*

I scored 95% in that exam. That day, I made a promise to myself: **never memorize blindly — understand, learn, and push forward. The results will follow.** This has been my life motto ever since.

## The bicycle that taught me self-reliance

Growing up, money was always tight — sometimes we could barely find 10 rupees. I always wanted a bicycle but could never afford one, so I borrowed friends' bicycles to learn. After much pleading, my father promised me one if I scored above 500 in my 10th standard exams. I scored 499.

A year later, my father finally managed to get me a bicycle. But I fell sick and was bedridden for over a month. When I finally rode it — just five days later, it was stolen.

That day I decided: **I will never ask anyone for anything. I will earn it myself. What you earn stays with you — what comes free, doesn't.**

## Engineering against all odds

My father wanted me to become a teacher, but something inside me always said *engineer*. I told him: either I do engineering, or I stay home and he has to feed me. Luckily, a friend of his stepped in, gave money for the entrance exam, and I cleared it.

That year, the government introduced fee reimbursement for low-income families and expanded engineering seats. I got into Electronics and Communication Engineering. Friends of my father helped with building fees and books. Government scholarships covered my exams.

From the 3rd year, I started working part-time to pay my own way. I also became a self-appointed lecturer — teaching topics like Analog and Digital Communications, Control Systems, Embedded Systems, Digital Signal Processing, Microprocessors, and more to my classmates, with professors' support. I graduated with 69%.

## The scariest bet of my life

After graduation, I discovered CDAC (Centre for Development of Advanced Computing) offered a PG Diploma in Embedded Systems — my dream field. But it cost ₹80,000. A family friend helped with ₹25,000 and my mother gave the only gold she had to secure a bank loan for the rest.

This was the scariest moment of my life. If I failed, that gold — our family's only emergency reserve — would be gone forever.

I moved to Kolkata, stayed with relatives to save money, and completed the program with **Grade A** (top grade). The bet paid off.
